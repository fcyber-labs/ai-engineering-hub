 STORY 1: Solar notches another win as Microsoft adds 475 MW to power its AI data centers.
-
Microsoft has signed a new agreement with AES to add 475 MW of solar capacity from three Midwest projects in Illinois, Michigan and Missouri, expanding its renewable‑powered portfolio for AI data centers. The deal follows earlier contracts, including a 389 MW solar purchase in February and a $9 billion renewable coalition organized by Acadia, bringing Microsoft’s own renewable assets to over 34 GW. Solar is attractive to tech firms because it can be installed quickly, is relatively inexpensive, and is modular, allowing rapid scaling to meet the surging power demand of AI workloads. To address solar’s intermittency, developers are increasingly pairing projects with battery storage, creating hybrid plants that are beginning to rival the cost of new natural‑gas generation. While nuclear power remains more expensive, its interest among tech companies persists, but renewables’ speed—utility‑scale solar can be online in about 18 months—gives them a clear advantage as new AI servers risk being under‑powered by 2027. Microsoft’s broader strategy includes a massive 10.5 GW renewable deal with Brookfield Asset Management, slated for delivery by 2030, underscoring the company’s commitment to decarbonizing its data‑center operations.

From powering Microsoft’s AI‑hungry data farms with clean solar energy, we now turn to another way AI is reshaping creative work—ElevenLabs is opening the doors for authors to produce and publish their own audiobooks straight from its platform.

 STORY 2: ElevenLabs now lets authors create and publish audiobooks on its own platform.
-
ElevenLabs has launched a publishing program that lets authors create and distribute AI‑generated audiobooks directly through the company’s Reader app, moving from a limited trial last year to an open rollout for all writers. The service is positioned as a low‑cost alternative to traditional studio production, with authors earning roughly $1.10 for each listener who engages with an audiobook for 11 minutes or more; test users averaged 19 minutes of listening. Initially the payout model is available only for English‑language titles in the United States, but ElevenLabs plans to extend it to the 32 languages it supports. The company also intends to build a marketplace where creators can sell their audiobooks, keeping more revenue for authors compared with platforms like Audible. This expansion follows a recent $180 million funding round and a partnership with Spotify to deliver AI‑narrated audiobooks. ElevenLabs’ AI voice tools are offered on subscription plans from $11 to $330 a month, significantly cheaper than hiring voice talent and studio time. The move signals the startup’s broader strategy to become a full‑stack publishing and distribution platform for indie audio content.

Speaking of AI‑driven content creation, the ripple effects are showing up on the biggest video stage of all: YouTube’s latest AI suite adds auto‑dubbing, age‑identification tools, and more, changing how creators and viewers experience media.

 STORY 3: YouTube AI updates include auto dubbing expansion, age ID tech, and more.
-
YouTube CEO Neal Mohan announced that AI is one of the platform’s four “big bets” for 2025, highlighting a suite of new creator tools. Starting this month, auto‑dubbing will let any YouTube Partner Program creator instantly translate videos into multiple languages, while AI‑generated image, background and music features for Shorts have already been released. The company is also deploying machine‑learning to estimate users’ ages so it can serve age‑appropriate recommendations, though the exact methodology was not disclosed. To protect creators, YouTube is expanding its AI‑detection capabilities—including a broader pilot with Creative Artists Agency and an upgrade to Content ID that can spot AI‑synthesized faces or voices. While some worry that AI‑generated content could dilute quality, YouTube frames the technology as a productivity aid rather than a creative replacement. Beyond AI, Mohan’s 2025 roadmap emphasizes positioning YouTube as a cultural hub, treating top creators as a new Hollywood, and shifting focus to TV screens, which now outpace mobile in U.S. viewership.

While AI is fine‑tuning the way we watch and listen, it’s also making waves in industries far beyond entertainment—Exxon is jumping head‑first into the AI gold rush, betting on the technology to overhaul its energy operations.

 STORY 4: Exxon can’t resist the AI power gold rush.
-
Exxon Mobil announced plans to build a 1.5‑gigawatt natural‑gas power plant dedicated to supplying electricity for AI data centers, operating as a fully islanded facility with no grid connection. The company says it will capture and store more than 90 % of the plant’s carbon dioxide, leveraging tax credits from the Inflation Reduction Act to offset the high cost of carbon‑capture and storage (CCS). Exxon aims to have the plant operational within five years, a faster timeline than most nuclear projects that tech firms are also courting for clean power. The move underscores the looming power shortfall for AI, with estimates that nearly half of new AI data centers could lack sufficient electricity by 2027. However, Exxon will face stiff competition from rapidly deployable, cheaper renewable projects such as Google’s $20 billion renewable portfolio slated for 2026 and Microsoft’s $5 billion, 9‑GW renewable initiative. The article highlights that while CCS technology is still maturing—few plants achieve their capture targets—Exxon hopes its investment will position it as a key energy supplier for the AI boom.

And that surge of corporate AI interest is feeding a broader funding frenzy, as seen with Ricursive Intelligence pulling in $335 million at a $4 billion valuation in just four months, underscoring how quickly AI‑focused startups are attracting massive capital.

 STORY 5: How Ricursive Intelligence raised $335M at a $4B valuation in 4 months.
-
Anna Goldie (CEO) and Azalia Mirhoseini (CTO), former Google Brain and Anthropic engineers who created the Alpha Chip that automated TPU design, have founded Ricursive Intelligence. Leveraging their AI‑driven chip‑layout technology, Ricursive builds software that speeds up the design of any silicon chip rather than manufacturing chips themselves. Four months after launch, the startup closed a $300 million Series A round at a $4 billion valuation, led by Lightspeed, following a $35 million seed round from Sequoia. Nvidia, AMD, Intel and other chip makers are its target customers, while Nvidia even participates as an investor. The founders’ unusually synchronized careers—joining and leaving companies on the same days—highlight their deep partnership. Their earlier Alpha Chip work proved the concept but also sparked internal controversy at Google, underscoring the high stakes of AI‑accelerated chip design. Ricursive aims to make chip design a fast, automated process, potentially cutting years of human effort down to hours.
